About me

Hi there, I'm Emily

I'm a software development consultant and career-changer.

I kickstarted my tech career with roles at two different financial institutions before moving on to work on some internal projects with FDM Group.

While I've previously worked with Java, Spring, Spring Boot, React and Angular, I'm currently exploring the Python ecosystem.

I consider myself first and foremost to be a developer, but I'm also interested in data and cloud, and therefore open to roles in those spaces.

Why tech?

I studied Modern Languages at the University of Sheffield, and while it was a good experience, I never felt that it was the 'right' degree for me. Admittedly, computing lessons, when I was at school, weren't what they are nowadays, so computer science never crossed my mind as a subject to study at a higher level. The 'closest' I got was Maths A Level (which is hardly Computer Science is it). Especially given that the one module that might have been somewhat useful (Decision Mathematics) as the one module my school didn't offer!!

I'm a self-confessed 'geek'. I love learning, problem-solving, and experimenting with new technologies. I find joy in crafting solutions to complex problems, and from my perspective, the world of tech provides the perfect canvas for my creative coding endeavours.

The fact that it is impossible to know every programming language, library and framework out there, makes it all the more exciting

Above all, the potential to work on innovative projects, while using technology to solve real-world problems, contributing to projects that have a positive impact on people's lives around the world, is what most excites me about working in tech.

I enjoy attending tech events; this year I attended a two-day, all-female hackathon, Athena Hack, organised by ShowCode. as well as the launch event of the JavaScript framework Qwik.

I'm an advocate for mental health and women in technology.

While much great work has been done to attract women into technology, such as the many initiatives of Code First Girls, only 24% of developer positions are held by women, and many of those still have a STEM background, as opposed to a humanities or arts degree. Far too women *still* face various forms of discrimination within the tech space; there's still much ground to cover in the journey toward true equality.
